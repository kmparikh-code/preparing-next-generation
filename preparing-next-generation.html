The Problem Most Wealthy Parents Face

You've worked hard to build wealth. You've made smart decisions, saved consistently, and planned carefully.

But here's what keeps a lot of parents up at night: What happens when this wealth transfers to your kids?

72% According to a 2024 Citizens Bank survey of 1,500 Americans, 72% don't feel confident in their ability to manage a financial windfall.

It's not because kids are irresponsible. It's because most families never prepare them.

They assume kids will "figure it out" when the time comes. They worry that talking about money too early will ruin their work ethic. They fear the conversation will be awkward.

So they wait. And often, they wait too long.

The truth: Preparing the next generation isn't about teaching them to manage a portfolio. It's about instilling values, building competence, and creating a shared understanding of what wealth means to your family.

3 Things You Need to Know About Preparing Heirs

1. "The Money Talk" Should Start Way Earlier Than You Think

‚ñº

Most parents wait until their kids are in their 20s or 30s to start talking about family wealth. By then, you've already missed the most important years.

The reality: Kids start forming beliefs about money around age 7. By their early teens, many of those beliefs are set.

That doesn't mean you need to tell your 10-year-old about your net worth. But you do need to start teaching basic concepts: saving, delayed gratification, the difference between wants and needs, and most importantly, the values your family has around money.

Waiting until they're adults means you're not teaching them - you're correcting them. And that's a much harder conversation.

Start age-appropriate conversations early. Build on them as your kids grow. By the time they're ready to inherit, money won't be a mysterious or scary topic - it'll be something they've been learning about their entire lives.

2. Your Estate Plan Isn't a Wealth Transfer Plan

‚ñº

A lot of families think they're "prepared" because they have an estate plan. Trusts are set up. Beneficiaries are named. Documents are signed.

But an estate plan just tells the money where to go. It doesn't prepare your kids to receive it.

Think about it this way: If you were handing your kids the keys to a car, you'd make sure they knew how to drive first, right? You wouldn't just throw them the keys and hope for the best.

Wealth is the same way. The legal structure matters, but the preparation matters more.

That means teaching them about investments, taxes, and cash flow management. Involving them in family financial discussions. Letting them make small financial decisions (and mistakes) while the stakes are low. Explaining the "why" behind your wealth transfer decisions.

Your estate plan is step one. Your wealth education plan is step two. You need both.

3. Your Kids Are Watching (Even When You Think They're Not)

‚ñº

You might think your kids don't notice how you handle money. But they do. Every single day.

They watch how you tip at restaurants. How you talk about your job. Whether you stress about bills or feel confident. How you react when something breaks. Whether you comparison shop or impulse buy. How you handle unexpected expenses.

They're absorbing lessons about money constantly - whether you're teaching them or not.

And here's the thing: What they observe will shape them more than anything you say in a formal "money talk" years later.

If you want your kids to be responsible with money, you can't just tell them to be responsible. You have to show them what that looks like. That means being intentional about the financial behaviors you're modeling - and occasionally narrating your thought process so they understand why you make the choices you do.

The good news? You're already teaching them about money every day. The question is: What lessons are they learning?

What to Discuss at Every Age

Click on each age range to see what to teach:

Ages 5-10: Foundation

‚ñº

At this stage, money is simple and tangible. Focus on teaching that money has value and that choices have consequences. Kids this age are ready to understand the basics without feeling overwhelmed.

  • Teach delayed gratification through saving for small goals
  • Explain the difference between needs and wants
  • Let them earn money through age-appropriate chores
  • Show them how to save, spend, and share (charitable giving)
  • Model positive money behaviors in everyday situations

Ages 11-15: Building Understanding

‚ñº

Middle school years are perfect for introducing banking basics and the concept of growing wealth. They're old enough to grasp more complex ideas but still receptive to guidance. Start involving them in real family financial discussions at an age-appropriate level.

  • Open a checking account and teach them how to manage it
  • Explain interest, compound growth, and basic investing concepts
  • Involve them in family discussions about vacation budgets or large purchases
  • Introduce the concept of different types of income (earned vs. passive)
  • Teach them about taxes and how money is earned

Ages 16-18: Developing Responsibility

‚ñº

High school years are crucial for hands-on financial responsibility. Let them make real decisions and even mistakes while the stakes are still relatively low. This is also when they can start meeting your financial advisor to understand what that relationship looks like.

  • Help them manage their first paycheck and create a budget
  • Teach them about taxes, deductions, and reading a pay stub
  • Discuss student loans, scholarships, and college financing options
  • Introduce them to your financial advisor for college planning discussions
  • Let them practice making spending vs. saving decisions on their own
  • Explain credit, credit scores, and responsible borrowing

Ages 18-25: Active Learning

‚ñº

Young adulthood is when financial decisions start having long-term consequences. They're making career choices, possibly taking on student debt, and beginning to save for retirement. This is when their relationship with your advisor should become active and ongoing, not just occasional check-ins.

  • Help them understand 401(k)s, Roth IRAs, and early retirement savings
  • Discuss student loan repayment strategies and refinancing options
  • Bring them to advisor meetings to learn about investment strategies
  • Start introducing the broader family wealth picture (without overwhelming them)
  • Teach them about insurance, emergency funds, and financial safety nets
  • Help them navigate early career financial decisions and salary negotiations

Ages 25-35: Growing Involvement

‚ñº

By their late 20s and 30s, your children should be active participants in family wealth conversations. They're likely established in their careers, possibly married, and ready to understand more about your estate plan and investment philosophy. Regular advisor meetings become essential.

  • Include them in regular meetings with your financial advisor
  • Explain your investment philosophy and why you make certain choices
  • Start discussing your estate plan, trusts, and how wealth will transfer
  • Involve them in family decisions about real estate, business interests, or major assets
  • Teach them about tax planning, charitable giving strategies, and wealth preservation
  • Help them establish their own financial advisory relationships if needed

Ages 35+: Preparing for Leadership

‚ñº

By this stage, your adult children should have full transparency about family wealth and an active role in major financial decisions. They need to be prepared to work with advisors independently and, eventually, make decisions about the family's financial future on their own.

  • Provide complete transparency about the size and structure of family wealth
  • Give them an active role in family investment and estate planning decisions
  • Ensure they have their own relationships with your advisors (not just as "your kids")
  • Discuss succession planning for family businesses or leadership roles
  • Teach them how to evaluate advisors and make decisions about changing strategies
  • Help them develop their own wealth transfer plans for the next generation
